From Drifting to Showing Up: What This Vinternship Gave Me:

Before this Vinternship, I wasn’t exactly struggling.  
I was just drifting.

I knew coding basics from school and college. I wasn’t new to it. But I wasn’t moving forward either. Days passed in doom scrolling and wasted time, always telling myself I’d start “soon.” I was curious—but lazy. Doing things without direction, learning without commitment.

When this Vinternship began, I assumed it would be like others—attend sessions, complete tasks, move on. But over these two weeks, something quietly shifted. I started showing up. Standup sessions, meetings, discussions—I didn’t miss any. Slowly, my days began to feel structured, not forced.

The learning phase on ViBe came with weekly milestones and deadlines. That changed things. The deadlines weren’t overwhelming, but they were enough to keep me accountable. On days when motivation was low, the milestone still waited. I remember one day rewatching the same MERN video three times, coding alongside it slowly, because I didn’t want to move ahead without understanding. It took time—but it felt honest.

The MERN stack itself was challenging. It was new for me. I had basic knowledge of HTML and CSS, but this required deeper thinking. I’m not someone who watches and moves on. I need to pause, understand, and code along. Because of that, I moved slower than many others. At times, that made me feel like I was behind.

But I chose to trust my pace.

Whenever things got frustrating, I didn’t quit. I stepped back when my mind felt overloaded, took short breaks, and returned with clarity. I rewatched videos, coded line by line alongside Nathan Prabhudesai sir, and made sure I understood before moving forward. Progress wasn’t fast—but it was real. This experience reinforced something important for me: finishing early means very little if understanding is missing.

Listening to Professor Sudharshan sir during sessions was another eye-opener. Even a few sessions with him made a strong impact. His clarity, positivity, and perspective stayed with me. One quote in particular—“Your network is your net worth”—felt real during this Vinternship. I met people, made friends, shared doubts, and realized how much learning improves when you’re not doing it alone.

The case studies felt like sweet headaches. They were difficult—but in a good way. There was no single correct answer waiting at the end. You had to think, reason, and sit with uncertainty. That discomfort was frustrating at times, but it made the learning feel genuine.

I haven’t completed the entire Vinternship yet. I’m still in the learning phase, and I haven’t faced the viva. But even now, I can say this clearly: I’m not the same person who started. The biggest change isn’t how much I know—it’s how I approach learning. I’m more patient with myself and more willing to stay with difficulty instead of escaping it.

This Vinternship didn’t suddenly make me an expert.

But it did something more important.

It made me show up—again and again.

And that’s where real growth begins.
